Why I Deleted All My Social Media

Why I Deleted All My Social Media | Choosing to Give My Attention Back to Myself

Social media drained my focus, my thoughts, and my sense of real connection.
It wasn’t until I let it go that I finally found myself again.

A few months ago, I deleted all of my social media accounts.
No farewell post, no dramatic announcement—just silence. I only kept Gmail as my way of staying in touch.

It wasn’t an impulsive decision. It was the result of months of inner struggle, a choice made for healing and for reclaiming my focus.

I Cared Too Much About People Who Didn’t Care About Me

At one point, I was spending nearly eight hours a day on Instagram.
I checked who posted a story, who replied to my messages, who ignored me, whose life looked better than mine.

It wasn’t their fault—it was mine.
I cared too much about people who never cared back.

This over-caring left me mentally exhausted.
I thought I was “maintaining connections,” but in reality, I was letting algorithms hijack my attention in exchange for emptiness.

I Asked Myself: Is This Information Changing Me?

One day, I sat down and asked myself:

If the information I consume daily shapes my thoughts,
then what are these endless scrolls doing to me?

The questions piled up:

  • Am I letting my brain be filled with fragments of noise?
  • Am I replacing real thinking with shallow topics?
  • Am I mistaking fake, polished lives for “normal”?
  • Has my way of speaking become restless and hollow because of this?
  • Has my self-image been twisted by the content I consume?

The answers were terrifying. Almost all of them were yes.

Choosing to Keep Only What Feeds Me

I made a deal with myself: if I’m going to spend one hour each day consuming content, then I must consciously choose what it is.

So I divided information into two categories:

Worthwhile, nourishing content:

  • Thoughtful podcasts (knowledge, inspiration)
  • Insightful YouTube channels (documentaries, culture, education)
  • Articles and blogs that share honest perspectives
  • Beautifully crafted games (where design, sound, and story matter)

Low-quality distractions to avoid:

  • AI-generated endless short videos
  • “Cotton candy” content that gives dopamine but no depth
  • Shallow Instagram posts of curated lifestyles
  • Empty viral gimmicks with no creativity

This simple filter redefined what counts as leisure and what is simply wasted time.

I Found Back My Words—and Myself

After deleting social media, I felt lighter.
No more notifications to check every minute. No more envy or insecurity from watching others.

Instead, I now enjoy deep conversations with two or three people who truly matter. I find joy in books, blogs, writing, learning, and exercising.

There was a time when I stopped reading, and my vocabulary shrank until I felt almost speechless.
But now, I’ve reclaimed my language, my focus—most importantly, myself.

A Reminder: Your Attention Is Your Most Precious Resource

I’m not saying you should delete all your accounts today.
But I do invite you to ask yourself this:

“Does what I scroll through every day actually make me better?”

If the answer is no, then maybe it’s time to give your attention—and your life—back to yourself.

Q&A: Common Questions About Quitting Social Media

Q1: What if I miss something important?
Don’t worry. Truly important things will reach you.
If the world were collapsing tomorrow, your friends, family, or coworkers wouldn’t rely on Instagram to tell you.

Q2: Will my friendships disappear without IG or Facebook?
No. Real friendships don’t vanish with a deleted account.
Those who care about you will still find you. The ones who don’t? They weren’t truly close to begin with.

Q3: How do I stay connected without social media?
By reaching out intentionally. I now use Gmail, LINE, or SMS for people who matter. I schedule calls, write emails, or meet in person. These connections feel more real than likes and stories ever did.

Q4: Won’t I feel lonely?
You’ll feel quiet, not lonely.
At first, it’s strange because we’re used to filling every silence with noise. But soon you realize—loneliness isn’t the lack of information, it’s the inability to be with yourself. Once you start reading, writing, exercising, or creating, you’ll find freedom.

Q5: Will I miss out on trends or news?
Not really. In fact, I’m more focused now. I choose what I consume: newsletters, podcasts, and selected sites. Instead of being force-fed by algorithms, I practice information subtraction. That gives me clarity and control.

👉 Your attention is your life. Don’t waste it on things that don’t deserve it.
